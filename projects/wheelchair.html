Multi-Element High-Lift System Design & CFD Optimization

Master’s research project focused on the aerodynamic design, CFD analysis, and shape optimization of a three-element high-lift configuration for a regional transport aircraft. The goal was to maximize L/D during take-off and landing while respecting structural and actuation constraints.

Performed at Technische Universität Braunschweig under supervision of Prof. Dr.-Ing. Rolf Radespiel and Dr. Vlad Ciobaca at the Institute of Fluid Mechanics.

My Role

  • 2D/3D CFD setup (ANSYS Fluent & OpenFOAM)
  • Mesh generation & grid convergence studies
  • Shape parameterization (FFD + Hicks-Henne)
  • Adjoint-based and surrogate-based optimization
  • Validation against wind-tunnel data
  • Documentation & conference presentation

Tools & Methods

The Challenge

Modern regional aircraft require excellent high-lift performance during take-off and landing while keeping mechanical complexity and weight low. Current slat/flap settings are often based on decades-old designs with room for improvement using modern CFD and optimization tools.

Key constraints:

  • Maximum lift coefficient ≥ 3.2 at α = 16°
  • Stall angle ≥ 18°
  • Fixed slat & flap deflection angles (20°/30°)
  • Gap & overlap limited by actuation mechanism
  • No flow separation on main element

Reliable CFD Simulations

Structured O-grid with y+ < 1, >35 layers in boundary layer. Grid convergence study showed < 1% difference in C_L between medium and fine mesh. Validation against DLR-F15 high-lift wind-tunnel data yielded excellent agreement in pressure distribution and lift curve.

Optimization Outcome

After 84 design iterations using SU2 adjoint method + Kriging surrogate:

  • +11.8 % increase in C_L,max>
  • +9.2 % increase in L/D at C_L = 2.6
  • Stall angle increased from 17.1° → 19.4°
  • Reduced separation bubble on flap upper surface

Final Optimized Configuration

Optimized slat & flap positioning with modified flap trailing-edge contour eliminated flow separation and delayed stall.
